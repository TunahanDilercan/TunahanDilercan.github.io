Resume Guide

A practical, skimmable reference on formatting, ATS, bullet writing (STAR/XYZ/CAR), content strategy, and accessibility.

Introduction

Your resume is a 30-second elevator pitch on paper. It should clearly convey your contact info, education, experience, and background—and convince the hiring team to interview you.

Know your audience. Craft your resume strategically so it parses well and highlights what matters to the role. Ultimately, substance matters: real, relevant experience wins.

For students, use college time wisely: build projects, join engineering teams, participate in research, and get internships. These add meaningful substance to your resume.

General Rules

  • Make it easy to read and skim; one column. Avoid icons/images/graphics.
  • Use bullet points, not paragraphs; don’t indent sections/bullets.
  • Use a modern, easy-to-read font (Calibri, Bitstream Charter, Arial). Black text, 10.5+ size.
  • Do not justify text; ensure sufficient white space and margins (≥ 0.4 inches).
  • Avoid excessive italics, bold, and ALL CAPS; use separation and hierarchy consistently.
  • One page unless 10+ years of experience (≈1 page/decade).
  • Separate skills using commas; keep sections clearly distinct.

Make Your Resume More Effective

  • Include relevant technical details; use STAR/CAR/XYZ methods for bullets.
  • Provide context and relevant keywords; avoid fluff.
  • Prioritize impactful details at the top/left for scanning.

Keep it to 1 Page

Most resumes don’t need a second page. Clean formatting and focused content make one page sufficient. If you truly need more space (senior/staff level), ensure every line earns its place.

Formatting & ATS

  • Ensure it’s easily skimmable and parsable by ATS.
  • Avoid complex layouts and graphics that hinder parsing.

Accessibility

  • Use readable fonts (Calibri, Charter, Arial, Lato, Helvetica) with sufficient contrast.
  • No justified text; avoid grey/light fonts or ultra-thin weights.
  • Use clear section separation and consistent spacing between sections/jobs.
  • Margins ≥ 0.4 inches; avoid indentation beyond bullets.

Dates

  • Use “Present” for current roles. If using months, use them consistently.
  • Don’t abbreviate years (use 2023). Prefer en dashes with spaces: Mar 2022 – Jun 2024.
  • Right-align dates; bullets shouldn’t extend beyond dates.

Section Order

  • Graduated w/ FT role: Work Experience > Skills > Education (or Skills > Work > Education).
  • Student/new grad: Education > Work > Skills (or Education > Work > Projects > Skills).
  • No tech work experience: Education > Projects > Work > Skills.
  • Avoid summaries unless senior/career change/gap; no references section.

Contact Information

  • Optional: security clearance; citizenship/work authorization if relevant.
  • Omit physical address and often phone number; use a single professional email.
  • Write URLs in plain text (no brackets/labels); no https://www. prefixed.
  • Avoid empty GitHub/portfolio links.

Work Experience

  • Title the section “Work Experience” or “Experience.”
  • Include paid work and research; mark internships/contract roles clearly.
  • Order roles and bullets by relevance/impressiveness; tailor to the job.
  • Describe accomplishments, not just duties. Add context, impact, and metrics.

Bullet Points

Objectives

  • Highlight technical work, challenges solved, and impact.

General Rules

  • 1–2 lines per bullet; one sentence each. No first-person pronouns.
  • No ending periods; avoid wrapping a bullet to the next line with 1–4 trailing words.
  • Use digits (8, not “eight”); avoid excessive adjectives/adverbs.

Action Verbs

Start bullets with strong, past-tense verbs (analyzed, designed, implemented, optimized…). Avoid weak or misused verbs (utilize, leverage, etc.).

Methods: STAR / XYZ / CAR

  • STAR: Situation, Task, Action, Result
  • XYZ: Accomplished X as measured by Y by doing Z
  • CAR: Challenge, Action, Result

Quantify results and move metrics early in the bullet when possible.

Why Integration Matters

Don’t list parts; explain how you integrated components to achieve outcomes. Focus on interfaces, constraints, validation, and results.

Education

  • Reverse chronological order; no coursework unless highly specialized/relevant.
  • Use graduation date only (e.g., May 2025). Consider listing GPA if ≥ 3.75.
  • Remove GPA after first FT role unless exceptional.

Skills

Objective: concisely highlight technical skills aligned to the job. Avoid buzzword dumps.

Do

  • Include languages/technologies you can discuss confidently; mirror in bullets.
  • Use 3 lines or less; single-column; order by importance; comma-separated.
  • Group into sensible categories (Languages, Tools, Design, etc.).

Don’t

  • No soft skills, trivial tools, or multi-column layouts.
  • No proficiency labels (Expert/Intermediate); show via bullets.
  • Avoid “C/C++”; prefer “C, C++”.

Projects

  • Use “Projects” as the section title; avoid redundant word “project” in titles.
  • For personal projects, focus on links and bullets (not roles/locations/dates).
  • Order by relevance/impressiveness; include real, useful, maintained work.
  • Publish projects with strong READMEs, screenshots, run/test instructions, and tests.

Bias

Avoid adding personal details (age, gender, etc.) that can introduce bias in screening.

Senior Engineers (10+ YoE)

  • Consider a brief summary; have separate resumes for management vs. IC tracks.
  • Aim for ≤ 2 pages; emphasize impact and influence; streamline early career items.
  • Move education to the end.

Career Changers

  • Add a short summary explaining the change and motivation.
  • Link working projects/source code; be concise with prior experience.
  • Keep it to 1 page when possible.

Grammar, Punctuation, and Spelling

  • Spell out obscure abbreviations on first use.
  • Run grammar as well as spell checks; use tools (Grammarly, Hemingway, LanguageTool, Quillbot).
  • Ask others to proofread; typos imply lack of attention to detail.

Networking Matters

Applications alone often aren’t enough. Network actively, follow up on submissions, and consider adjusting your search scope over time. Create a timeline and iterate.
